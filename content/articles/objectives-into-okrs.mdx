Every analytics engagement I run opens with the same blunt question: **why do you
have a website?** It sounds almost rude. It's the most useful question in the
room.

Most answers start vague — "to grow the business," "to be online" — and that's
fine. The job is to take that fuzzy intent and sharpen it into something you can
actually measure and act on. That's what OKRs are for, and getting them right is
[the first stage of the whole analytics cycle](/articles/the-analytics-cycle):
skip it, and everything downstream measures the wrong things beautifully.

<KeyTakeaways>
  <Takeaway>An objective is the destination; key results are how you know you arrived.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Good key results are numbers with a target and a deadline — not activities.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>If a metric doesn't ladder up to a key result, you probably shouldn't track it.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Three objectives, three key results each. More than that and nothing is a priority.</Takeaway>
</KeyTakeaways>

## Objectives vs. key results

The two halves do different jobs, and teams constantly blur them.

An **objective** is qualitative and a little ambitious — where you want to be. It
should read like something you'd actually say out loud: *"Make first-time buyers
want to come back."*

A **key result** is the evidence. It's a number, with a starting point, a target,
and a date. *"Lift 90-day repeat-purchase rate from 18% to 25% by Q4."* If you
can't argue about whether you hit it, it isn't a key result yet.

<Callout type="warning" title="The vanity-metric trap">
  "Grow traffic" feels like a goal. It isn't. Traffic with no link to revenue or
  retention is a number that goes up while the business stands still. Always ask:
  if this metric doubled tomorrow, would the business actually be better off?
</Callout>

## Building one, step by step

Here's the sequence I walk a client through. It takes an afternoon, not a
quarter.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Answer the blunt question">
    Why does the site exist *this year*? Sell more to new customers? Get
    existing ones to come back? Cut the support tickets that swamp your inbox?
    Pick the one or two that matter most right now. You can't chase everything.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Write the objective in plain language">
    State the destination as a sentence a human would say. No metrics yet — just
    the ambition. "Turn one-time buyers into regulars" beats "increase LTV" every
    time, because everyone on the team understands it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Attach 2–3 key results">
    Now make it measurable. For each objective, find the two or three numbers
    that, if they moved, would prove you got there. Each gets a baseline, a
    target, and a date. If you don't know the baseline, that's your first
    measurement task.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Pressure-test against reality">
    Are the targets honest — a stretch, not a fantasy? Can you actually
    influence them, or are they at the mercy of a Black Friday you don't control?
    Trim anything you can't move or measure.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Write down what you're *not* doing">
    The discarded goals matter as much as the chosen ones. Naming them stops the
    team from quietly drifting back to measuring everything.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## A worked example

Say you run a coffee-subscription store. The blunt question surfaces the real
goal: too many people try one bag and vanish. So:

> **Objective:** Make first-time buyers want a second bag.
>
> - **KR1:** Lift 60-day repeat-purchase rate from 21% → 30% by end of Q3.
> - **KR2:** Grow subscription sign-ups as a share of orders from 12% → 20%.
> - **KR3:** Cut "where's my order?" support tickets per 100 orders from 9 → 4.

Notice what just happened. You now know exactly what to instrument: repeat
purchases, subscription conversion, post-purchase support volume. Every one of
those is a thread you can pull in the next stage of the cycle. The OKRs didn't
just set goals — they wrote your measurement plan for you.

<Callout type="tip" title="Keep it on one page">
  If your OKRs don't fit on a single page, they're a wish list, not a plan. Three
  objectives is plenty for a quarter. Ruthlessness here is a feature.
</Callout>

## What this unlocks

Once the OKRs exist, the rest of the analytics cycle stops being guesswork. You
implement tracking *for these numbers*. You build dashboards *around these
numbers*. You test changes *aimed at these numbers*. The endless "what should we
even look at?" debate evaporates, because you decided up front.

That's the quiet power of starting with the blunt question. It doesn't just give
you goals — it makes every decision that follows obvious.
