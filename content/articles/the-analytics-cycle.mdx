Most analytics work fails for a boring reason: it starts in the wrong place. A
team buys a tool, turns on tracking, and ends up with a mountain of data nobody
can connect to a decision. The dashboards look busy. Nothing changes.

The fix isn't more data. It's running analytics as a **cycle** — where each
stage exists to feed the next, and the whole thing loops back on itself.

<KeyTakeaways>
  <Takeaway>Start with the business question, not the tool.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Track intent, not just outcomes — it tells you *why*.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Dashboards point at the problem; tests prove the fix.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Every deep-dive surfaces the next set of goals. The loop never really ends.</Takeaway>
</KeyTakeaways>

## The cycle, in five stages

<Steps>
  <Step title="Understand your objectives">
    It starts with one question: *why do you have a website?* Sell product?
    Generate leads? Reduce support load? We pin that down and turn it into
    OKRs — concrete objectives with measurable key results. If a metric doesn't
    ladder up to one of these, we don't bother tracking it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Implement analytics">
    Now we measure — your KPIs, plus the **signals of user intent** that explain
    them. Outcomes tell you what happened; intent tells you why. I'm
    platform-agnostic here: bring your own stack, or I'll recommend one. The
    point is a clean, trustworthy implementation aimed at the goals from step one.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build reports & dashboards">
    This is where it starts to *feel* like analytics. We turn raw events into
    clear reports and dashboards — in the tools your team already uses — so the
    state of the business is legible at a glance, not buried in an export.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Test &amp; optimise">
    Good dashboards do something underrated: they show you where the journey
    breaks. From there we test improvements, measure the lift honestly, and keep
    only what actually works.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Deep-dive analyses">
    With data flowing and the basics solid, we dig in — the analyses that find
    where to move the needle next. These almost always surface new goals worth
    chasing.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip" title="Why the order matters">
  You can't optimise what you can't see, you can't see what you didn't
  implement, and you shouldn't implement anything you can't tie back to a goal.
  Skip a stage and the later ones quietly stop working.
</Callout>

## Why it's a loop, not a line

That last stage is the important one. A deep-dive doesn't just answer a
question — it changes what you're trying to do. You learn that your best
customers behave differently than you assumed, or that a "small" segment drives
most of the profit. Those insights become **new objectives**, and you're back at
step one with a sharper question than you started with.

> The goal was never a finished dashboard. It's a business that gets a little
> smarter about itself every quarter.

That compounding is the whole point. The first loop sets up the measurement. The
second one starts paying for itself. By the third, you're making decisions your
competitors are still guessing at.

## Where most stores are stuck

Most teams I meet are stranded between stages two and three — plenty of tracking,
some dashboards, but no clear line back to objectives and no habit of testing.
They're data-rich and decision-poor. The cycle is how you get unstuck: not by
collecting more, but by closing the loop.
