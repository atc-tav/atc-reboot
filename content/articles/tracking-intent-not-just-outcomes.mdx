Most stores measure outcomes obsessively: orders, revenue, conversion rate. All
necessary. All, on their own, useless for deciding what to do next.

An outcome tells you *what* happened — conversion fell to 1.8%. It says nothing
about *why*, and "why" is the only thing you can act on. To get there you have to
measure the layer underneath: the **signals of user intent** that lead up to the
outcome. That's the heart of [stage two of the analytics
cycle](/articles/the-analytics-cycle) — implementing tracking that explains, not
just counts.

<KeyTakeaways>
  <Takeaway>Outcomes are lagging; intent signals are leading. You can steer by the leading ones.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Every outcome has a chain of intent behind it — instrument the chain, not just the end.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Track the moments where intent is highest *and* most fragile, like checkout.</Takeaway>
  <Takeaway>Be deliberate: a few well-chosen signals beat a firehose of events nobody reads.</Takeaway>
</KeyTakeaways>

## Outcomes vs. intent

Think of it as two layers. The outcome is the result. The intent signals are the
behaviours that reveal what a visitor was *trying* to do on the way there.

A drop in conversion is an outcome. But underneath it sits a chain: people
searched, viewed products, added to cart, reached checkout, started entering an
address. Each step is a signal of intent — and a drop-off between any two of them
tells you exactly where the wheels came off. The outcome says "we lost sales."
The intent chain says "we lost them at the shipping step." Only one of those is
something you can fix on Monday.

<Callout type="note" title="A quick gut-check">
  For any number on your dashboard, ask: is this telling me *what happened* or
  *why*? You need both layers. Most stores have a wall of the first and almost
  none of the second.
</Callout>

## The signals worth capturing

You don't need to track everything — you need to track the moments that reveal
intent and the moments where it's most likely to break. A practical starting set
for ecommerce:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Discovery intent">
    Site search terms (especially the ones returning no results), category
    browsing depth, filter usage. These tell you what people came looking for —
    and whether you actually have it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Consideration intent">
    Product-detail engagement: image zooms, size-guide opens, reviews read,
    repeat visits to the same product. High consideration with low conversion is
    a loud signal that something — price, trust, clarity — is in the way.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Purchase intent">
    Add-to-cart, cart views, checkout starts, and crucially the step-by-step
    progress *through* checkout. This is the highest-intent, most-fragile stretch
    of the whole journey. Instrument every step.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Friction signals">
    Form-field errors, repeated coupon attempts, rage-clicks, back-and-forth
    between steps. These are intent meeting an obstacle — the richest fixes
    usually hide here.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Tie every signal back to a goal

This is where it's easy to go wrong. The temptation is to turn on every event a
tool offers and sort it out later. Don't. An event you collect but never look at
is a liability — it clutters reports, slows pages, and creates the *illusion* of
measurement.

<Callout type="warning" title="More tracking isn't better tracking">
  A firehose of unmapped events is how teams end up data-rich and decision-poor.
  Each signal should trace back to a key result from your OKRs. If it doesn't,
  leave it off.
</Callout>

The discipline is the same one we set up in
[stage one](/articles/objectives-into-okrs): if a signal doesn't help explain a
number you actually care about, you don't need it. Tracking *fewer* things, well,
beats tracking everything, badly.

## Stay platform-agnostic

People expect me to arrive with a favourite tool. I don't. GA4, a warehouse-first
setup, a product-analytics platform, server-side tagging — they're all just ways
to capture the same signals. The right one depends on your stack, your team, and
your budget, not on what I happen to like.

What matters far more than the tool is the *thinking* underneath: which signals,
mapped to which goals, captured cleanly enough to trust. Get that right and the
tool becomes an implementation detail. Get it wrong and the fanciest platform on
the market just gives you prettier noise.

With both layers flowing — outcomes *and* the intent behind them — you've got
everything the next stage needs. That's when the dashboards start telling you not
just how the store is doing, but what to do about it.
