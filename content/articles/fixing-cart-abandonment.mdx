Cart abandonment gets talked about like it's one problem. It isn't. It's a dozen
small frictions stacked on top of each other, and most of them are fixable in an
afternoon. This is the order I work through them.

<KeyTakeaways points={[
  "Measure where people actually drop, don't guess.",
  "Fix mobile checkout first — that's where most revenue leaks.",
  "Unexpected costs are the number one reason carts get abandoned.",
  "Test one change at a time so you know what worked.",
]} />

## Start with the numbers, not the opinions

Before touching anything, pull the last 90 days and find the real drop-off
points. You're looking for the single biggest gap between two steps.

<StatGrid>
  <Stat value="69.8%" label="Average cart abandonment" />
  <Stat value="−42%" label="Typical lift from fixing checkout" />
  <Stat value="3 steps" label="Ideal max checkout length" />
</StatGrid>

Industry averages are a useful sanity check, but your funnel is the only one
that matters. If your mobile checkout converts at half your desktop rate, that's
your project.

<Callout type="tip" title="Quick win">
  Adding a free-shipping threshold (e.g. "Free shipping over $75") almost always
  lifts both conversion and average order value at the same time.
</Callout>

## The fix, in order

Work top-down. Don't jump to the shiny stuff before the basics are solid.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Show total cost early">
    Surprise shipping and taxes at the final step are the number one reason
    people bail. Put the full price in front of them before checkout starts.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Make mobile checkout effortless">
    Big tap targets, autofill, numeric keyboards for card fields, and as few
    screens as you can manage. Most of your traffic is on a phone.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Offer guest checkout">
    Forcing account creation is pure friction. Let people buy first and create
    an account after, if at all.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add trust where money changes hands">
    Security badges, a clear returns policy, and recognisable payment options
    right next to the buy button.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't do this">
  Resist the urge to redesign the whole checkout at once. You'll have no idea
  which change moved the number — and you'll probably break something.
</Callout>

## Measure, then move on

Change one thing, give it enough traffic to mean something, and compare against
your baseline. Then go to the next item on the list.

> The goal isn't a perfect checkout. It's a checkout that loses less money than
> it did last month — every month.

That's the whole game: small, measured improvements, compounding over time.
